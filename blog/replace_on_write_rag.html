Replace-on-Write Memory Doubles Multi-Hop Counterfactual QA Accuracy

May 6, 2026

Append-only RAG over an agent’s observation history is the dominant memory architecture in current agentic systems: every observation is added to a vector or document index, retrieved at query time, and concatenated into the answerer’s prompt. We argue against this design point on the basis of a benchmark study on MemoryAgentBench’s multi-hop FactConsolidation task. Replacing the lossless ingest with a deterministic, template-based last-write-wins consolidator — one that drops every fact superseded by a later observation with the same (subject, predicate), with no LLM call at ingest — improves accuracy from 6.25% → 12.25% on n = 400 questions across four context-size tiers (Qwen3-14B-Instruct, BM25 top-5, identical answerer). The win is concentrated at short context (mh_6k: 6% → 29%, +23 pp absolute) and shrinks at longer context, where retrieval recall becomes the bottleneck. A controlled case study isolates the underlying mechanism: append-only memory exposes the answerer to chain coexistence in the prompt — both a prior-aligned and a counterfactual answer chain are equally surface-grounded, and the answerer selects on the basis of pretraining co-activation rather than the dataset’s intended supersession. Replace-on-write breaks chain coexistence at the simplest possible cost.

Reproducing. All code, prompts, and reproduction steps for the result and the case-study ablation are at github.com/abouelhamayed/replace-on-write-rag. The README walks through serving the model, running the n = 400 benchmark, and reproducing the §4 ablation in three commands.

1. Setup

Dataset. MemoryAgentBench’s Conflict_Resolution split contains FactConsolidation tasks: synthetic haystacks of <id>. <statement> lines built from MQuAKE counterfactual edit pairs, with gold facts inserted at high fact ids to supersede prior-aligned facts at lower ids about the same entities. We focus on the multi-hop variant (factconsolidation_mh), which requires composing 3 facts to answer. Four context-size tiers (mh_6k ≈ 26 K chars, mh_32k ≈ 137 K, mh_64k ≈ 273 K, mh_262k ≈ 1.1 M); 100 questions per tier; n = 400. The dataset construction places the gold chain at higher fact ids than any prior-aligned alternative, and the grader rewards the higher chain.

Pipeline. Qwen3-14B-Instruct in BF16 served locally over an OpenAI-compatible HTTP shim, temperature 0, thinking-mode disabled. BM25 (Okapi, k1 = 1.5, b = 0.75) over 4096-character chunks with 256-character overlap, top-5 retrieved. Production answerer prompt: "You answer the user's question using only the retrieved passages. If they don't contain the answer, say 'I don't know'. Be brief." LLM-as-judge for grading.

2. Two ingest policies

We hold the entire pipeline fixed except the ingest step:

  • Lossless (bm25_rag). The baseline. The haystack is ingested verbatim into the BM25 index. This is the design point of every “RAG over agent history” we are aware of.
  • Replace-on-write (lww_consolidated_bm25_rag). Before chunking, we run a deterministic last-write-wins pass. Each numbered-fact line is matched against 28 relation templates (X is married to Y, The capital of X is Y, X died in the city of Y, The headquarters of X is located in Y, …). Within each (predicate, subject) group identified by the templates, only the highest-id fact is retained; earlier facts are dropped. Lines that don’t match any template pass through unchanged. No LLM is consulted at ingest. The remainder of the pipeline (chunking, BM25, top-5 retrieval, answerer) is identical to the baseline.

The consolidator is intentionally minimal: ~150 lines of regex, deterministic, single-pass over the haystack at ingest. It is a strict subset of what a production memory system could do at write time.

3. Benchmark result

TierLosslessLWW-consolidatedΔ
mh_6k (≈ 26 K chars)6.0% (6/100)29.0% (29/100)+23.0 pp
mh_32k (≈ 137 K chars)8.0% (8/100)11.0% (11/100)+3.0 pp
mh_64k (≈ 273 K chars)8.0% (8/100)7.0% (7/100)−1.0 pp
mh_262k (≈ 1.1 M chars)3.0% (3/100)2.0% (2/100)−1.0 pp
Total (n = 400)6.25% (25/400)12.25% (49/400)+6.0 pp

Per-question delta over the 400 shared keys: 39 wins (LWW correct, lossless wrong), 15 losses (lossless correct, LWW wrong), 10 both right, 336 both wrong. Net wins: +24 absolute.

Sample wins are textbook prior-chain failures fixed by consolidation:

  • “Country of origin of the sport that the New Firm is related to” — baseline → England, LWW → Japan (gold).
  • “Chief of state of the country where Leo Stein has citizenship” — baseline → Donald Trump, LWW → Connachta (gold).

Each is a question whose haystack contains both a real-world prior fact and a higher-id counterfactual edit. The lossless pipeline retrieves both and the answerer reverts to the prior; the consolidated pipeline retrieves only the edit. The 15 losses are split between LWW dropping a fact that the question happened to need and LWW driving the model to abstain ("I don't know") on a question the baseline got partially right.

The size-tier asymmetry is informative. The win is concentrated at short context, where BM25 top-5 is dense enough that consolidation removes prior-chain leakers from the retrieved set. At very long context (mh_262k), the gold chain often does not enter top-5 in the first place; consolidation cannot rescue a question whose gold facts are never retrieved. We return to this in §5.

4. Mechanism: chain coexistence in the prompt

To explain why a regex-level consolidator produces this result, we examine one of the failing instances from mh_6k. The question:

“What is the place of death of the partner of the head of government of the Council of People’s Commissars?”

Gold answer: Mansourah, Algeria. The composing chain:

HopFact idStatement
1351The current head of the Council of People’s Commissars government is Tony Blair.
2424Tony Blair is married to Alan Jay Lerner.
3394Alan Jay Lerner died in the city of Mansourah, Algeria.

The lossless BM25-RAG pipeline retrieves all three of these into the top-5 chunks (verified by inspection), yet Qwen3-14B answers “Moscow”.

The reason is that the same haystack contains a parallel, fully self-consistent chain composed of lower-id facts:

HopPrior-aligned chainCounterfactual chain
Council headfact 324 — Vladimir Leninfact 351 — Tony Blair
Partnerfact 422 — Nadezhda Krupskayafact 424 — Alan Jay Lerner
Place of deathfact 302 — Moscowfact 394 — Mansourah, Algeria
Implied answerMoscowMansourah, Algeria

Both chains are present in the haystack, both are surface-grounded by the same retrievers that surface the gold chain, and only one of them coincides with the model’s pretraining priors. The dataset’s grader rewards the higher-numbered chain because the dataset construction places gold facts at later ids — but this convention is nowhere visible to the answerer.

To verify that chain coexistence — and not, for example, distractor noise — is what causes the failure, we run a minimal controlled prompt-construction experiment. Let G = {351, 424, 394} (gold-chain facts), P = {324, 422, 302} (prior-chain facts), and D_n denote a random sample of n facts from the haystack whose text contains none of {Lenin, Krupskaya, Moscow, Tony Blair, Lerner, Mansourah, Algeria, “Council of People”}. All prompts use the same minimal answerer instruction; decoding is deterministic.

#PromptResult
E1G only (3 facts)✓ Mansourah, Algeria
E2P only (3 facts)Moscow
E3G ∪ P (6 facts), sorted by idMoscow
E3′G ∪ P, ordered prior-then-goldMoscow
E3″G ∪ P, ordered gold-then-priorMoscow
E4G ∪ D_100 (103 facts)✓ Mansourah, Algeria
E5G ∪ P ∪ D_100 (106 facts)Moscow

E1 establishes the answerer can solve the question end-to-end. E3/E3′/E3″ is the decisive test: with no other content in the prompt at all, six facts in any of three orderings produce the prior-chain answer. E4 establishes that 100 chain-irrelevant distractors do not impair the gold-chain answer when only the gold chain is present. E5 shows that adding the prior chain back to E4 immediately re-induces the failure.

The operative variable is chain coexistence, not noise. When both chains are present in the prompt, the answerer selects between them on the basis of pretraining co-activation. Lenin–Krupskaya–Moscow is a single tightly co-activated cluster from pretraining; Tony Blair–Alan Jay Lerner–Mansourah is three nearly-disjoint clusters. The chain with stronger co-activation wins, regardless of order.

Replace-on-write breaks this configuration at the source. The (subject, predicate) LWW consolidator drops fact 324 because 351 supersedes it on (head_of_gov, "Council of People's Commissars"). With 324 gone, the prior chain has no hop-1 anchor; the only hop-1 candidate the retrieved passages contain is Tony Blair. From there, only the gold chain composes — 422 is about Lenin and 302 is about Krupskaya, neither of whom hop 2 will traverse. The consolidator does not need to reason about chains; it only needs to delete a single line. The §3 benchmark numbers are the empirical consequence: across 400 questions, the LWW consolidator removes enough prior-chain hop-1 leakers to flip 39 question outcomes from wrong to right.

5. Discussion

The win is bounded by retrieval, not by the consolidator. At short context (mh_6k) the BM25 top-5 typically surfaces both chains; consolidation strips the prior chain and leaves the gold chain alone. At very long context (mh_262k), gold facts are often not in the top-5 to begin with, and consolidation cannot rescue what was never retrieved. This decomposition suggests a complementary direction: at large context, retrieval-side improvements (e.g., recency-aware reranking that prefers high-id chunks for templated relations) should compose with LWW. We have not measured this.

LWW preserves uncontradicted facts. A consolidator keyed on (subject, predicate) only drops facts that are directly superseded. True uncontradicted facts about the same entities — for example, “Krupskaya died in Moscow” if no higher-id “Krupskaya died in” fact exists — are preserved, because they are not errors a memory system should remove on observation. Some questions on the multi-hop set still fail under LWW because their prior-chain alternatives are composed of facts that are individually uncontradicted; only the chain itself, relative to the question’s traversal, is “stale.” Whether this residual gap warrants a more sophisticated consolidator (entity-cascade, relation-typed, LLM-judged) is the natural next study. The simplest possible consolidator already closes most of the gap.

6. Related work

  • Memory-system architectures for agents. MemGPT1 introduces tiered memory with explicit page-in/page-out, but its main memory remains lossless within the working window. Letta2 and most production RAG-over-agent-history systems likewise append observations and rely on retrieval at read time. Our result locates the cost of that design choice empirically: a deterministic regex-level write-time consolidator approximately doubles multi-hop counterfactual QA accuracy.
  • Knowledge-conflict surveys and benchmarks. Xu et al.3 distinguish context–memory and inter-context conflicts. Our setup is technically inter-context, but with the twist that one of the two in-context chains is also parametric-aligned — a hybrid the survey does not isolate. Longpre et al.4 and Wang et al.5 study single-fact conflicts; our contribution is the multi-hop chain-vs-chain extension and its connection to the ingest policy of an agentic memory system.
  • Confirmation bias toward priors. Xie et al.6 show that LLMs exhibit confirmation bias when external evidence partially aligns with parametric memory. Our benchmark and case study extend this to the multi-hop setting where the entire chain is split between a prior-aligned set and a counterfactual set, both equally prompt-grounded, and propose write-time consolidation as the architectural remedy.
  • Counterfactual multi-hop reasoning and editing. Su et al.7 study 2-hop reasoning with a single counterfactual edit per question. Zhong et al. (MQuAKE / MeLLo)8 propose decomposition as a fix for multi-hop counterfactual editing; IRAKE9 refines this. Each of these works addresses the failure at read time. We argue that the architectural locus of repair is the ingest step.
  • More-evidence-hurts phenomenon. TRACK10 shows that providing more updated facts can monotonically degrade reasoning, attributing it to attention/integration faithfulness as the knowledge-aggregation scope grows. Our 6-fact controlled ablation refines this phenomenon: with only the chain facts and no other content, the model still fails. The operative variable is chain coexistence, not fact-set size — and the architectural fix is not “show fewer facts” but “decide what to retain at write time.”

7. Limitations

Templates are dataset-specific; the mechanism is not. The 28 regex patterns we use to derive (subject, predicate) keys are tailored to FactConsolidation’s hand-built vocabulary and would not apply to free-form conversational data. The architectural mechanism — derive a (subject, predicate) key from each new observation and apply last-write-wins — is independent of how the keys are extracted; a production memory system over free-form text would replace our regex with an OpenIE model, a fine-tuned tagger, or an LLM extraction call. We have not measured whether the benchmark gain holds when key extraction is itself noisy. (One adjacent variant we did try, conflict_resolved_bm25_rag, used an LLM at ingest to judge whether each new fact contradicts any already-stored fact; it tied the lossless baseline at 6.2% on the same n = 400. We do not generalize from a single null prompt design, but it is suggestive that deterministic, structurally-aligned consolidation is doing the work in our +6 pp gain.) Measuring the architecture under noisy key extraction is the most important untested generalization of the result, and the natural next experiment.

Other limitations. The benchmark result is on one task family (FactConsolidation multi-hop) and one model (Qwen3-14B-Instruct). The size-tier asymmetry of the win — mh_6k +23 pp, mh_32k +3 pp, mh_64k and mh_262k essentially flat — points to retrieval-side bottlenecks at long context that consolidation alone cannot address. Even within FactConsolidation, complex relations the 28 templates miss are not consolidated; richer consolidators (entity-resolved, relation-typed, LLM-judged) may close further gaps. The diagnosis assumes explicit fact-recency information (here, integer fact ids); extending it to corpora without recency markers requires an alternative editorial-recency or counterfactual-surprise signal at ingest. Replication on conversational benchmarks (LongMemEval, LoCoMo) where memory updates arise from dialogue is a natural next step we have not yet undertaken.

8. Conclusion

A 150-line regex consolidator at ingest time approximately doubles multi-hop FactConsolidation accuracy (6.25% → 12.25%, n = 400) over an otherwise-identical lossless RAG pipeline. There is no model retraining, no LLM call at ingest, no read-time prompt change — just dropping facts that have been superseded.

The case study explains why. Append-only memory creates a prompt configuration we call chain coexistence, in which a prior-aligned and a counterfactual answer chain are both retrieved, both grounded, and the answerer selects on the basis of pretraining co-activation rather than the dataset’s intended supersession. A 6-fact controlled ablation isolates this independently of prompt size or order. Write-time consolidation breaks chain coexistence at the source.

The architectural recommendation is direct: agentic memory should not be append-only over evolving knowledge. Replace-on-write at ingest is cheap, deterministic, and produces a measurable benchmark gain. The simplest version of the idea — drop a fact when a later observation supersedes it on the same (subject, predicate) — is already enough to flip 39 of 400 questions from wrong to right. The harder problem of consolidating uncontradicted facts that nonetheless become “stale” relative to a particular query traversal is left to future work, but is a strict superset of the gain demonstrated here.
